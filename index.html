**2025 Quality Systems and Practices Strategic Direction #2.** **Report Writing Guidelines for Teachers** Student reports should be personalised and comprehensive. They should provide students and parents with detailed, clear and specific information about student learning, growth, next steps and improvement measures. Comments should include personalised, constructive statements, the students achievement and progress (overall or in specific tasks), effort and participation in the KLA/subject/course and at least one area for improvement and how it can be achieved. A suggested paragraph scaffold is: 1. What the student has achieved (outcomes/tasks) a positive comment. 2. Their effort and participation in the course. 3. Where they need to focus/improve/develop and how they can make this improvement. In order to streamline our student report writing system, and provide consistency, please use the following guidelines: --- ### 1. Audience The report is written for, and to, parents. Addressing the report to the student is not appropriate. The report should be written in plain English, be in the third person and easy for parents to understand. It should present information in clear, jargon-free language. --- ### 2. Length of Comments Report comments should be at least three sentences long, with a recommended maximum of 150 words (Max 1000 characters). Generally, sentences should be kept short and no longer than 15 to 20 words. --- ### 3. Contradictions All care should be taken to align comments with marks, outcomes and student profile rankings. It is important that class teachers double-check this aspect: contradictions lead to a lack of credibility. **Examples of contradictions:** * a Jasmine has excellent reading skills. Reading Outcome: Limited * b If Jason completed his homework regularly, his results would improve. Homework completed: Always --- ### 4. First Names The students official name must be used in comments as their report is a legal document. Anglicised versions, shortened names and nicknames are not to be used. **Example:** Samuel has achieved… (not Sam) --- ### 5. Subject Names * The name of a school subject should begin with a capital letter. *e.g. Jason works well in History.* * When a comment is made about a students aptitude for a field of study or a concept, there is no capitalisation. *e.g. Jasmine is very interested in learning about history.* * Subject names should not be abbreviated *e.g. Mathematics not Maths* --- ### 6. General Punctuation * a Remember to use commas where necessary. * b Use an exclamation mark when adding sentence fragments at the end of a comment. *e.g. An excellent result!* * c The use of capital letters and numerical figures will apply in the following examples: **Correct usage:** * Preliminary Course or Stage 4 Visual Arts * Examination (capitalised) * Specific examinations: Half-Yearly Examination, Yearly Examination, Trial HSC Examination * Specific assessment tasks: First Fleet Assessment Task * Topic/module titles: *Australia as a Nation* (history), *Exploring places* (geography) **Incorrect capitalisation:** * No capitals in: *first term*, *second semester*, *assessment task*, *exams* when used generally *e.g. Robert enjoyed working on the student research task this term.* * d Report writing should be in a formal style. Avoid using contractions. *e.g. Jason did not complete (not didnt), has not (not hasnt) completed.* --- ### 7. Agreed Spellings * a program (not programme) * b co-operative (not cooperative) * c focused * d targeted * e syllabuses * f much-valued * g Organise, synthesise (use English spelling) * h class work * i homework * j well-mannered --- ### 8. Using the Conditional (“if”) Avoid making predictions about future performance; use words like **could** or **may** instead of **should, shall, can** or **will**. **Example:** *If Jason completed his homework on a regular basis, his marks could improve (not can improve).* Comments should not promise that if the student does what is recommended, then they will improve. --- ### 10. Avoid Clichés * i In order to improve, Jason must pull up his socks. * ii Jasmine is finally getting the hang of Computer Technology. * iii This term has been a real learning curve for Jason. --- ### 11. Homophones * **practise** (verb) / **practice** (noun) *Jason must practise his computer skills.* *With more practice, Jasmine will develop her computer skills.* * **affect** (verb) / **effect** (noun) *Jasons inconsistent application to class tasks will affect his results.* *Jasmines mark for this outstanding project has had a pleasing effect on her overall results.* * **there** (a place) / **their** (ownership) * **quite** (adverb) / **quiet** (adjective) *Jason works quite well in class.* *Jasmine is a quiet student.* * **been** (past participle) / **being** (present participle) *Jason has been an active participant in class discussion.* *Jasmine is being a constant distraction in class.* --- ### 12. Subject / Verb Agreement Single subjects = single verbs. Plural subjects = plural verbs. **Examples:** * *Jasmines assignment mark was very high.* * *Jasons assignment mark and his test result were both very high.* More examples: * *His attitude in class and his sudden request for more homework are pleasing to note.* * *Jasons attention to detail and neatness is very pleasing.* --- ### 13.
